The Veil Unbound · Act I · Session 12

The Duskmire Sanctum

Where the rot remembers what the gods forgot
Party Level: 6  ·  Players: 5  ·  Tone: Dark Fantasy Horror / Heroic Epic  ·  Stakes: Campaign-Defining
Session Overview

The party wakes rested in the Shattered Spire's Sanctuary Room at Level 6, the Sundered Diamond and Ember Shard finally theirs. They climb down, strike out northwest, and find a hamlet clinging to the edge of the Duskmire swamp — where they learn that two strangers came through days ago, bound for the exact place the party is headed. In the drowned ruins of a sunken village and its half-submerged temple, they uncover what Agares has been building toward all along. They will arrive, explore, be tested — and they will almost certainly be too late.

Primary Goal: Reach the Duskmire Sanctum's inner chamber. Witness — or interrupt — Agares' ascension rite. Walk out changed.

Secondary Goal: Barnabas and Fippy each find a thread in this place that ties to them personally.

Narrative Engine: The party is always a half-step behind. Every delay tightens the clock — because of what they choose, not because the DM says so. Cause and effect, all the way down.

Scene 1 — The Sanctuary Room
01
The Weight of Level Six
Roleplay / Transition

The Sanctuary Room keeps its quiet like a held breath. Bare stone walls, a floor scrubbed clean of the tower's corruption, and the low hum of a ward some long-dead hand pressed into the threshold. You slept the whole night through, no dreams — the first true rest since you set foot on the Spire's lowest stair.

The Sundered Diamond waits in its open case, right where you left it: a fist-sized stone, raw and uncut, throwing off a warmth the cold rock around it has no business allowing. Beside it the Ember Shard turns its two sparks in a slow, patient orbit. Thessavra's screams have faded down to memory. The tower overhead is gutted, empty, dead.

You are rested, and you are not the people who climbed in here. The work of this place has set into you the way tempering sets into a blade — and you can feel the new weight of yourselves when you stand.

DM Note — Opening Beat
Give them the moment. Let each player mark the level-up however they like, in character. The Sundered Diamond starts the conversation on its own — three real choices, mutually exclusive. Don't steer them toward one; the tension is the reward. After 10–15 minutes, point them toward the descent. The air smells of rain and rot. The swamp lies northwest.
Table Roleplay — The Quiet Hour
This is the only unhurried hour the party gets all session. Don't rush them out of it — spend it.
Go around the table. Each player names one small, private thing their character does now that the climb is over: a wound finally tended, a whispered prayer, a letter that never got sent, a long look at someone they nearly lost up there.
Put the Sundered Diamond on the table — literally, a token if you've got one. Ask the question out loud: if it came to it, who would each of you bring back? Let them argue. Let them not agree.
Whoever wants the diamond has to say why, to the others' faces. Whoever doesn't want it spent has to say that too.
The Sundered Diamond — Case Contents
The Sundered Diamond
A fist-sized raw diamond, uncut, radiating faint warmth. Ancient — locked deliberately by scholars who knew exactly what it was. Three uses, mutually exclusive:
— Sell It
Worth 1,500gp to the right buyer (arcane scholar, noble collector, major temple). ~800gp on the open market.
— Resurrection Component
Counts as the full material component for Raise Dead or Resurrection. Fully consumed on use.
— Speak with Dead (1 use)
Attune to the diamond. Cast Speak with Dead once, no components required. Diamond then goes cold and dark — retains ~700gp sale value.
Why Did the Scholars Have This?
The diamond sat in an identified case — they knew what it was. So why did scholars working a corrupted celestial tower keep a resurrection-grade diamond under lock and key? Were they bracing for casualties? Guarding something irreplaceable? You don't have to answer. But the players may ask, and the question is worth leaving open.
Scene 2 — The Descent
02
What the Tower Leaves You With
Exploration

The climb down through the Shattered Spire passes without a fight. The mechanical spiders lie where they fell. But the tower still has things to show you on the way out — arcane residue smearing the air in certain doorways, a scholar's notes curling off a rotted corkboard.

The fourth-floor kitchen stops you. Someone set this table, once. Six plates, three chairs — they always meant to drag in more chairs — and cups gone to grey dust. A duty roster hangs on the chalkboard. The last entry carries a date and then breaks off mid-word, as if the writer was called away and never made it back.

Outside, the dead land runs flat to every horizon. Northwest, the ground starts to turn: greener, lower, wetter. You can't see the swamp yet. You can smell it from here — standing water and slow, patient rot.

The Duty Roster
One name on the roster shares a surname with the portrait the party found — "please, for Markus, tell him I'm sorry." Pure flavor continuity. No player needs to catch it. But if they do, honor it.
Scene 3 — Thornmere
03
The Hamlet at the Edge of the Rot
Social

Thornmere is not quite a village. It's twelve buildings, a well, and a streak of stubbornness. These people have lived on the lip of the Duskmire for three generations. They aren't afraid of it — they're careful of it, the way you're careful of a neighbour whose dog bites. And they're suspicious of travellers who ask about the Sanctum, because travellers who ask about the Sanctum always bring trouble in behind them.

Thornmere reaches you as a smell before it's a place — woodsmoke, and under it something sour and fermenting. Then the buildings climb out of the tree line: a knot of low, grey-thatched structures, built plain and built to last, hunched at the edge of ground that cooperates a little less with every step north.

A woman splits wood by the well. She stops when she sees you. She doesn't run, doesn't call out — she just watches, axe loose in one hand, with the flat patience of someone who got over being frightened of things a long while ago and now simply takes their measure.

Along the south wall of the cottage sprawls a deep herb garden, a dense, half-wild tangle of plants and vegetables and stranger things you'd have to crouch down to name. It's the garden of someone who has been growing things for forty years and stopped, long ago, arranging them for any eye but her own.

Maret Dunhollow
Thornmere's self-appointed caretaker. 60s, Velastra human. Wears the permanent scowl of someone who has seen enough.
Voice: Economical. Minimum words. Silence is a tool. She trades information — she doesn't give it away.
What she knows: "Two strangers, four days back. One was tall, dressed too well for these parts, with a smile that meant something was wrong with the teeth behind it. Wore red. His companion never said a word. They asked after the old path to the Sanctum. I told them there is no safe path. They laughed. They went anyway."
On the Sanctum: "That place has been sinking since before my grandmother's grandmother could remember. The village that used to stand there — we don't say its name. The folk who raised that temple thought they'd found something worth worshipping. Oh, they found something."
On the old village: "The priests changed first. Started speaking about the worship different. Not the god they'd always served — something older. Something that was already watching back when watching had no name for itself. The village followed, because villages follow priests. The swamp followed, because the swamp follows everything."
What she gives: Directions to the firm path. And a warning: "If you hear singing — and you might — don't follow it. And whatever you do, don't answer back." She doesn't explain further.
Table Roleplay — Earning Maret's Words
Maret gives nothing for free. Make the party work the conversation, not the dice.
She answers in trade, not charity. Before she'll talk about the strangers in red, ask each player what their character actually offers — coin, a day's labour, news from the road, an honest answer to a question of her own.
The ward marker over her door is Barnabas's to raise. Let that player decide: ask in front of everyone, or hold it back? It's an order matter. Make sharing it cost something.
If the party gets grabby or rude, she simply stops talking and goes back to her wood. Reward patience here, not pressure.

Scene 4 — The Duskmire Approach
04
Through the Swamp
Travel / Hazard

Maret's path holds for the first hour. After that it turns into a suggestion, and then into something you keep walking only because you've decided to call it a path. Water climbs the roots of trees that have been dead so long they've forgotten what killed them.

The smell deepens — thick, organic, wrong. Not the sharp reek of fresh rot but something settled and ancient, the air of a place where something terrible happened a very long time ago and the land never closed over the wound.

Then the buildings begin. Or what the swamp left of them. Stone walls snapped off at the waterline. A chimney standing alone with no house left around it. A doorframe rooted in black water, opening onto nothing, still upright — as though it's waiting for the rest of the house to come back for it. This village didn't burn. It drowned. Slowly, inch by inch, year by year, until the swamp closed over the last roof like a mouth.

Swamp Hazard — Rot Sickness: The miasma of the Duskmire carries corrupted divine residue. Run this check twice — entering the village ruins, and entering the Sanctum. Each PC makes a DC 13 Constitution saving throw. Failure: Rot Fatigue — disadvantage on Constitution saving throws until a short rest outside the swamp or magical healing. Stacks.

Scene 5 — The Upper Sanctum
05
What Agares Left Behind
Exploration / Discovery

The Sanctum entrance is a broad stone arch, half of it drowned. Inside, the walls are old cut stone, worn smooth by generations of devotion — somebody loved this place, once, and you can read that love in the craftsmanship even as the corruption eats it alive. Beautiful stonework, furred all over with a creeping black mold that shifts, just barely, at the ragged edge of your torchlight.

Something lies on the ground ahead. Fabric. Red. A litham — a face-covering worn in certain traditions — and it has been torn, not unwound. Ripped away in haste, or in panic.

It is cut from the same cloth as the one Barnabas carries.

Barnabas — The Litham: He knows it on sight. The cloth, the cut, the precise knot of the ties — this is Ordo-issue. It belonged to one of his order. He doesn't know whose. Set that against the ward marker over Maret's door: someone from the Ordo came here, left a warning at the hamlet, walked into this Sanctum, and never walked out. The litham was torn off. There is no body.
Table Roleplay — A Brother's Cloth
Two hooks have now landed on Barnabas — the ward marker, and the torn red litham. Let it land in the room before the dice come out.
Hand the litham to Barnabas's player and let them describe what their character does with it. Pocket it? Hold it up for the others? Say a word over it?
Invite one other PC to ask Barnabas, in character, what the Ordo actually is and why a member of it would die out here. Let Barnabas decide how much truth to give.
Keep it short and let the silence sit. The party is somebody's last footsteps from here on in.

Scene 6 — The Deep Sanctum
06
The Inner Chamber
Climax

The stairs drop below the water table into chambers that ought to be flooded and aren't — the rite has shoved the water back and is holding it there. The air down here is warm and wrong. It carries sulfur, and under that something older: iron, incense, and the particular smell of a fire that has been burning for a very long time.

The stairs end at an archway, and the archway opens into the heart of it.

The inner chamber is circular, sixty feet across at a guess, the ceiling climbing up into a dark you can't see the top of. The floor is the original temple stone — worn glassy by centuries of kneeling — and carved into every inch of it, a ritual circle of impossible complexity. Hundreds of interlocking sigils.

At the center of that circle stands a man you recognize.

He isn't wearing red. He stands barefoot in robes of deep charcoal grey, arms slightly spread, eyes shut. The air around him turns without any wind to move it — slow currents of something invisible circling his body. At the four cardinal points, iron stands cradle bowls of burning black flame. The flames give off no heat. They burn, and they warm nothing.

His companion — a figure in grey, faceless, whose footsteps make no sound — stands at the circle's edge. Facing outward. Facing you.

⚠ DM Eyes Only — The Full Picture
Agares knows the party is here. He knew before they entered. He is approximately 15 minutes from completion when they arrive. This is the "too late" default.

His companion is a Cambion — half-fiend servant. Its function: delay. It will fight, negotiate, threaten, and buy time. It is not stupid.

Five-Fallow Naldri has been scrying this location via the coven's established watch. She observes. When Agares ascends, the power surge snaps her scry connection. She has what she needs. The coven now knows.

The Timing Track — Three Outcomes:

Early Arrival

The rite is interruptible. Dispatching the Cambion quickly and disrupting the circle (DC 16 Arcana, destroying one iron stand) collapses the rite. Agares is expelled, enraged, fights unstably. Escapes at 30% HP. A genuine win — at enormous cost.

On Time

The rite is at critical inflection. Agares fights while maintaining it. He achieves partial ascension — half the power, all the consequences. He leaves changed but not fully elevated. A partial outcome with major story implications.

Default — Too Late

The rite completes. Agares ascends. He turns to look at all of them, at once. He does not kill them. He says seven words and leaves. This is the intended outcome.

⚔ Encounter 3 — The Cambion at the Threshold
Deadly (Context-Dependent)

The Cambion is not the fight. The Cambion is the clock.

Running the Cambion

Private countdown: 6 rounds to completion in the "too late" track. Adjust based on party arrival state. If players try to bypass it to disrupt the circle, it grapples and delays.

The Cambion steps aside. Or drops. Or bolts. It doesn't matter which.

You are too late by the length of a single breath.

The circle catches — not with fire, but with meaning. Every sigil flares at once, every carved line of darkness snapping bright, and for one instant the whole pattern reads as a single word your mind refuses to keep hold of. The chamber makes a sound that isn't quite a sound — a pressure behind the teeth, a silence with weight to it, the held pause at the very bottom of a breath.

Agares opens his eyes.

They are darker than they were, and deeper, and there is something behind them now that wasn't there when you climbed down — something old and unhurried, settling in like a tenant taking a room it has waited centuries to claim.

He lowers his arms. He breathes once, slow. Then he turns and looks at you — at every one of you at the same time, his gaze finding all five faces at once though his head never turns that far. What's in his face isn't triumph. It's quieter than that. Recognition. The calm of a man watching a thing he predicted come true, dead on schedule.

He says seven words.

⚠ The Seven Words — Deliver Quietly
These are not a threat. Not a boast. A statement of fact, spoken by something that has stopped needing either. His voice carries a little further than it should in this enclosed space.

"You were always meant to witness this."

Then he is gone. Not teleported — teleporting takes effort, and effort is beneath what he's become. He is simply here, and then he is simply not. The circle's glow fades. The black flames gutter out. The chamber goes cold and quiet.

Scratched into the floor at the center of the circle, in still-warm stone: a partial symbol in a dead liturgical script. The party cannot read it. But Clementine, looking at one specific run of characters, feels a cold recognition she can't account for. She has seen three of those letters before. She doesn't know where. (DM: it is from her blue book's glowing script, Session 5. She has been carrying a fragment of Yssovare's mortal name without knowing it.)
Table Roleplay — After the Words
Hold the silence. Don't narrate the party's reaction for them — make them find it.
Around the table, one line each, in character, spoken into the cold of the emptied chamber. Some will rage. Some will say nothing at all. Let the nothing land just as hard.
Whoever fought hardest to stop the rite gets the first word, if they want it.
This is the beat they'll carry out of tonight. Resist the urge to paper over it with loot and mechanics — let them sit in being too late before you move on.
Agares — Post-Ascension State
Changed: Immune to banishment and compelled planar return. Can project corrupting influence at range without physical presence. Detectable by divine casters (DC 15 Religion) near sites of his influence — he is becoming perceivable in the way gods are perceivable. He will not disguise himself again. The next time the party sees him, he arrives as himself.

Long Game (DM Only): Agares does not know he is being cultivated by Yssovare. He believes his ascension was his own. He is wrong. The Sanctum's ancient devotion to Yssovare made this location a perfect vessel. Agares built his ascension on another god's altar. In time, that god will collect on what was used.
Scene 7 — What the Sanctum Leaves You With
07
Aftermath in the Deep
Denouement / Discovery

The chamber is cold now. The warmth the rite threw off went with the man who made it, and the water it held back has already begun, slow and patient, to weep through the walls again. You have maybe an hour before the deep sanctum starts to flood.

But the chamber still has its history to give. The walls down here are the oldest in the Sanctum, and here, in that oldest layer, the eye behind the veil is no longer just a symbol. It's a record. Story panels, carved in sequence — worn smooth in places, but legible if you take the time to look — showing something that happened in this very room a long, long time ago.

Getting Out
Transition

No further combat encounters — the Sanctum's wards are spent. There's only the long climb back up through the upper corridors, the wade through the flooded village ruins, and the slog back across the swamp to the firm path. Thornmere at sunset.

Maret doesn't ask what happened. She looks at you the way people who have watched travellers walk into the swamp and come back look at travellers who have walked into the swamp and come back. She steps aside. There's a fire going. There's food. She sets out more plates without being asked.

It's a long while before anyone says anything. Outside, the swamp makes its sounds. And somewhere down in the dark beneath all that rot and water, the story panels on the deep chamber walls go on telling their quiet testimony to no one who will ever come to read it.
